A Foundation for Universalisation in Games

→ Abstract

In revealed preference theory, observed choice is interpreted as revealing tastes over the outcomes of the decision. Nevertheless, if a moral principle prescribes an act for reasons unrelated to its consequences, the inference drawn regarding preferences is misleading. In this paper, I study the behaviour of deontological decision makers who follow the moral principle of universalisation. I develop a decision theory for agents who value the impact of their choice in determining a counterfactual outcome they envision. Hence, the choice of action reveals a preference for counterfactual outcomes. I propose a unifying model based on my theory, inspired by the equal sacrifice principle. It can be specified to obtain the most prominent models of universalisation, compare them, highlight and arguably overcome their limitations.

Can Pessimistic Beliefs Threaten Redistribution? (with Michele Bisceglia)

[Preliminary draft available upon request]

→ Abstract

We develop and test a theory of how beliefs about other people's responsiveness to incentives shape preferences for redistribution. In a linear income taxation model where agents have heterogeneous prosocial preferences, more altruistic agents distort their labour supply to a lesser extent when the tax rate is higher. If agents have imperfect information on (the distribution of) preferences in the society, overly pessimistic beliefs imply a relatively low Condorcet-winner tax, even though most agents are poor and/or altruist. We develop an experimental design to test this prediction.

Unchaining Chang’s Chaining: Either Irrelevant or False (with Annalisa Costella)

[Preliminary draft available upon request]

→ Abstract

We show that formalising Chang's chaining argument against the Trichotomy Thesis demonstrates that it is either superfluous or invalid. We develop two formal reconstructions of the argument. In the first, we assume that the set of elements to be ordered has no structure. In this framework, whether the Trichotomy Thesis is true is independent of the validity of the chaining argument. In the second, we put minimal conditions on the set that allow us to state the chaining argument so that it bears its conceptual meaning. In this framework, the argument is invalid, since its premises are inconsistent with each other. Our results illuminate that the existence of `unconventional' value relations cannot be established by endorsing or rejecting the chaining argument. As such, the chaining argument does not provide insights into axiology.
