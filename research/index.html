A Foundation for Universalisation in Games Draft available upon request.

→ Abstract

According to revealed preference theory, the choice of a decision maker "reveals" his tastes over possible outcomes. However, if an act is prescribed by a moral principle for reasons unrelated to the consequences it induces, the inference drawn regarding preferences is misleading. In this paper, I study the behaviour of deontological decision makers who follow the moral principle of universalisation. I develop a decision theory according to which agents value the impact of their choice in determining a counterfactual outcome they envision. Hence, an action can be interpreted as revealing a preference for counterfactual outcomes. I show that I can specify my framework to obtain the most prominent models of universalisation, compare them and highlight their limitations. I propose a unifying model of universalisation based on my decision theoretic framework that arguably overcomes these limitations.

Can Pessimistic Beliefs Threaten Redistribution? (with Michele Bisceglia) Preliminary draft available upon request.

→ Abstract

We develop and test a theory of how beliefs about people's responsiveness to incentives shape preferences for redistribution. Participants in a simple economy exhibit partial altruism towards the worse-off in the society. They vote on the tax rate and then work. The higher their altruism level, the more attenuated their labour supply response will be to an increase in the tax rate, as part of production goes to the worse-off. If an agent believes that others will react significantly to the lower incentives of more redistributive systems, she will favour less egalitarian policies. Due to pessimistic beliefs about other agents' altruism, a poor and/or relatively altruistic agent can vote for a lower tax rate than a rich and/or more selfish individual. On aggregate, if beliefs are overly pessimistic, there will be too little redistribution compared to what would emerge under complete information about preferences. We test the theory in a laboratory experiment.

Unchaining Chang’s Chaining: Either Irrelevant or False (with Annalisa Costella) Preliminary draft available upon request.

→ Abstract

We show that Chang's chaining argument is logically invalid. We do so by proposing two formal reconstructions of it. In the first, we assume that the set of elements to be ranked has no structure. In this setting, whether the trichotomy thesis is true is independent of the chaining argument. In the second reconstruction, we put the most minimal conditions on the set that allow us to state the chaining argument in a way that bears its conceptual meaning. In this setting, the argument is false, since its assumptions are inconsistent with each other. Our results demonstrate that the existence of `non-conventional' value relations cannot be established by endorsing or rejecting the chaining argument. The chaining argument does not provide insights into axiology.

Redistribution of Freedom: A formal approach (with Annalisa Costella)

→ Abstract

Economic policies not only affect welfare in a society but also freedom. In this paper, we propose a formal framework that allows analysing the effects of economic policies on individual freedom. We do so by delineating a class of ordinal freedom rankings on sets general enough that it can be specified to encompass different understandings of freedom in the philosophical literature. We show that these rankings have a functional representation by the means of a measure defined over classes of sets. The functional representation of the ordinal ranking allows the employment of freedom rankings in economic analysis. Our representation result relies on a so far neglected formal equivalence between qualitative probabilities in decision theory and freedom rankings. We exemplify the relevance of a functional representation of a freedom measure by drawing on a stylised example of a universal basic income policy and its effects on freedom. We show that, contrary to what has been argued, a tax scheme subsidising universal basic income does not always maximise the real freedom of the worst-off in a society.
